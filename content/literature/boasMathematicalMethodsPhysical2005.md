## chapter 1 - infinite series, power series {#chapter-1-infinite-series-power-series}


### the geometric series {#the-geometric-series}

a geometric progression is obtained by multiplying each term by some fixed
number to get the next. they can be written in the form:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:geometric-progression}
a + ar + ar^2 + \cdots + ar^{n - 1} + \cdots
\end{equation}

the sum of \\(n\\) terms of a geometric series is given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sum-n-terms-geo-prog}
S\_n = \frac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}
\end{equation}

then the sum of a geometric series (if there is one) is given by:

\begin{equation}
S = \lim\_{n\to\infty} S\_n
\end{equation}

this means that a geometric series has a sum if and only if \\(\lvert r \rvert \lt
1\\), given by

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sum-infinite-geo-prog}
S = \frac{a}{1 - r}
\end{equation}

the series is then called **convergent**.


#### using geometric progression to find fractions for repeating decimals {#using-geometric-progression-to-find-fractions-for-repeating-decimals}

using \eqref{eq:sum-infinite-geo-prog} we can find fractional representations of
